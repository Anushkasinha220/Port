1. Introduction
1.1 Background and Motivation
Artificial intelligence has become one of the most transformative forces in
modern medicine. Deep learning models, particularly Convolutional Neural
Networks (CNNs), have demonstrated radiologist-level performance on tasks
such as skin lesion classification, diabetic retinopathy grading, and chest X-ray
pathology detection.
However, the deployment of such models in real clinical settings faces a
persistent and critical obstacle: data scarcity for rare and underrepresented
diseases. Medical image datasets are inherently imbalanced. For common
conditions such as pneumonia or benign nevi, thousands of labelled images are
readily available. For rare diseases, including specific cancer subtypes,
uncommon retinal disorders, or rare blood pathologies, clinicians may have
access to only a few dozen confirmed cases.
This extreme class imbalance causes standard classifiers to exhibit high
accuracy on majority classes while failing on the very diseases that are most
critical to detect early. Traditional approaches to address imbalance, such as
oversampling, SMOTE, or geometric augmentation, produce images that are
trivial variations of existing samples and do not capture the true underlying data
distribution of the rare class. Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) offer a
fundamentally different solution: learning the joint distribution of images and
disease labels, then synthesising entirely new, clinically plausible images for
any target class.
1.2 Importance of Low-Code / No-Code Tools in Deep Learning
The democratisation of deep learning through platforms such as Google Colab,
Hugging Face Spaces, and MedicalAI Pro has lowered the barrier to entry
significantly. Researchers and clinicians who lack expertise in distributed
systems or GPU cluster management can now train, visualise, and evaluate
complex GAN architectures through interactive, browser-based interfaces.
These tools offer automated data ingestion pipelines, real-timetraining loss
visualisation, and one-click FID evaluation, capabilities that previously required
substantial engineering effort. For Problem Statement 3, leveraging such
platforms accelerates the iteration cycle and allows the researcher to focus on
architecture design and medical interpretation rather than infrastructure.

2. Topic Explored: GAN Variant Analysis
2.1 Description of cGAN Architecture and Innovation
A Conditional GAN (cGAN) was first proposed by Mirza and Osindero as an
extension of the original GAN framework. The key innovation is that both the
Generator and the Discriminator receive the class label as an additional
conditioning input alongside the image or noise vector. This allows the network
to generate images for a specific, controllable class rather than sampling from
an unconditional data distribution.
The architecture implemented in this project draws from three major advances
in conditional image generation:
● Projection Discriminator, where class conditioning is implemented as a dot
product between image features and the class embedding.
● Conditional Batch Normalization, where affine parameters are predicted from
the class embedding.
● Residual Generator Blocks with spectral normalization, inspired by BigGAN,
to improve training stability and output sharpness.
2.2 Theoretical Background: Generator vs. Discriminator Game
The cGAN training procedure is a two-player minimax game. The Generator
maps a latent noise vector sampled from a normal distribution and a class label
to a synthetic image. The Discriminator receives either a real image from the

--- PAGE 5 ---
training set or a synthetic image generated by the model, along with the
corresponding label, and outputs a scalar score indicating whether the input is
real or fake.
Training alternates between two updates per iteration. First, the Discriminator is
updated to maximise its ability to distinguish real from fake images for the
given class. Second, the Generator is updated to minimise the Discriminator’s
confidence in classifying its outputs as fake. Because both networks are
conditioned on the class label, the Generator must produce images that are not
only realistic but also class-specific.
Figure 1: cGAN overall architecture — Noise z and class label y are fused via learned embeddings, decoded by
G, and the Discriminator D classifies both real and synthetic images conditioned on y.
2.3 Mathematical Model: Loss Functions
This implementation uses the Hinge GAN loss combined with an R1 gradient
penalty and a perceptual loss term.
Discriminator Loss (Hinge)
The first term penalises the Discriminator when it fails to score real images
above +1. The second term penalises it when it fails to score fake images below
-1. This margin-based formulation is more stable than binary cross-entropy.
L_D = E[ReLU(1 − D(x, y))] + E[ReLU(1 + D(G(z,y), y))]
Generator Loss (Hinge + Perceptual)
The adversarial term encourages the Generator to produce images that the
Discriminator scores as real. The perceptual term improves structural fidelity.
L_G = −E[D(G(z,y), y)] + λ · L_perceptual

R1 Gradient Penalty
This term is applied to real images only every 16 training steps. It penalises
large gradients of the Discriminator at real data points and helps prevent

2.4 Relevance to Real-World Applications
Synthetic medical image generation addresses a genuine and urgent need in
clinical AI. Three direct applications are listed below:
● Data augmentation for rare disease classifiers by generating class-balanced
training sets.
● Privacy-preserving data sharing because synthetic images can reduce patient-
identifiable information exposure.
● Medical education and simulation through high-fidelity pathology image
generation for clinician training.
3. Problem Statement
3.1 Description of the Generative Task
The official problem statement is: “Develop a Conditional Generative
Adversarial Network (cGAN) to generate class-balanced medical images for
underrepresented diseases, and assess performance gains in automated diagnosis
models under low-data regimes.”
The generative task in this report is class-conditional medical image synthesis.
Given a disease class label, such as Melanoma, Basal Cell Carcinoma, or
Actinic Keratosis, the Generator must produce a 128 × 128 RGB image that is
visually and clinically similar to a real dermoscopic photograph of that
condition. The downstream task is multi-class skin lesion classification using a
ResNet-50 classifier across seven disease labels.

3.3 Why This Problem Was Chosen
This problem was chosen because it addresses a major practical barrier to
deploying clinical AI: the lack of labelled data for rare conditions. Diseases that
are rare are often among the most dangerous to miss. A model that achieves
high overall accuracy while ignoring rare classes is clinically unacceptable.
cGAN-based augmentation directly addresses this problem by enabling the
model to learn rare disease morphology from limited examples and then
extrapolate to generate diverse new samples.

4. Methodology and Training Strategy
4.1 Workflow / Steps Followed
1. Set up the environment using Python 3.10, PyTorch 2.x, PyTorch Lightning
2.x, and CUDA 11.8.
2. Acquire the dataset using the MedMNIST API with automatic download and
standardised splits.
3. Analyse class distribution and identify underrepresented classes below the
500-sample threshold.
4. Preprocess data by resizing to 128 × 128, normalising channels, and encoding
labels.
5. Use WeightedRandomSampler to construct balanced training batches.
6. Design the cGAN architecture with a residual Generator and a spectral-
normalised Discriminator.
7. Train for 200 epochs with mixed precision and alternating Discriminator /
Generator updates.
8. Generate 1,000 synthetic images per class after convergence.
9. Evaluate synthetic images using FID, IS, and SSIM.
10. Train and benchmark a ResNet-50 classifier under multiple low-data
fractions.

4.2 Model Architecture
Generator
The Generator receives a concatenated vector consisting of a 128-dimensional
Gaussian noise vector and a 128-dimensional learned class embedding,
producing a 256-dimensional input. A fully connected layer projects this input
into a 512 × 4 × 4 spatial feature map. Five residual upsampling blocks
progressively increase the resolution from 4 × 4 to 128 × 128.
Each block applies Conditional Batch Normalization, ensuring class-specific
texture generation at every spatial scale. The output layer uses a convolutional
layer followed by a Tanh activation to produce a three-channel RGB image in
the range [-1, 1].
Figure 2: Generator network — progressive upsampling from 4×4 latent grid to 128×128 RGB output,
conditioned at every block via Conditional Batch Normalization (CBN).

--- PAGE 10 ---
Discriminator
The Discriminator uses a PatchGAN-inspired architecture with five spectral-
normalised residual downsampling blocks, reducing the image from 128 × 128
to 4 × 4. Global sum pooling collapses the spatial dimensions. The final score is
computed as the sum of an unconditional linear term and a projection term
based on the class embedding.

4.4 Training Stability Strategies
The following strategies are used to improve GAN stability:
● Spectral Normalization on all Discriminator convolutions.
● R1 Gradient Penalty on real images every 16 steps.
● Two-Timescale Update Rule (TTUR) with learning rate for the Discriminator
four times that of the Generator.
● Label smoothing with real targets set to 0.9.
● Exponential Moving Average (EMA) of Generator weights for inference.
● WeightedRandomSampler to ensure balanced class presence during training.
4.5 Additional Manual Configurations
Additional manual configurations include progressive training from 64 × 64 to
128 × 128, Weights & Biases logging for sample monitoring, and checkpoint
saving of the top-performing Generator models.
5. Implementation Details
5.1 Data Upload / Preprocessing
Data acquisition is handled through the MedMNIST API, which provides
standardised, pre-split medical imaging benchmarks. The DermaMNIST dataset
is downloaded and cached locally. A custom preprocessing pipeline is then
applied.
● Resize all images from 28 × 28 to 128 × 128.
● Normalise pixel values to the range [-1, 1].
● Convert labels to single-label integers from 0 to 6.
● Use official train, validation, and test splits.
● Apply WeightedRandomSampler for balanced mini-batches.

5.4 Benchmarking Framework
The downstream benchmarking experiment follows a rigorous protocol to
isolate the effect of cGAN augmentation:
1. For each data fraction in {5%, 10%, 20%, 50%, 100%}, randomly subsample
real training images per class.
2. Train a baseline ResNet-50 on real data only.
3. Train an augmented ResNet-50 on real data plus 1,000 generated images per
class.
4. Repeat each experiment three times using different random seeds.

7. Challenges and Limitations
Several practical and methodological challenges were encountered during this
work:
● Training instability in the early epochs due to possible mode collapse.
● Higher FID for very rare classes with fewer than 200 training examples.
● Resolution limitation at 128 × 128 compared with higher-resolution clinical
dermoscopy images.
● Risk of synthetic data contamination if generated images are not kept
separate from the test set.
● FID alone does not guarantee diagnostic validity without expert clinical
review.

8. Conclusion and Future Work
This study demonstrates that a well-designed Conditional GAN can
substantially mitigate class imbalance in medical image datasets and improve
downstream diagnostic model performance. The cGAN trained on
DermaMNIST generates visually plausible skin lesion images across all seven
disease classes while improving macro-AUC and macro-F1 most strongly in
low-data regimes.
A central contribution of this work is the demonstrated improvement in data
efficiency, suggesting that cGAN-based augmentation can significantly reduce
the amount of costly labelled medical data required for training clinically useful
models.
Future work may include the following directions:
● Scaling to 256 × 256 and 512 × 512 resolutions using progressive growing or
StyleGAN2.
● Extending conditioning inputs to include patient metadata.
● Training the cGAN in a federated learning setting across hospitals.
● Replacing ResNet-50 with transformer-based architectures.
● Conducting blinded clinical validation with dermatologists
